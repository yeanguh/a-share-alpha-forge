# General Industry-Chain Analysis Methodology

Use this reference to keep the skill industry-neutral. Start from the user's research question, then choose the smallest useful analysis spine. Do not force every report into a fixed manufacturing-heavy template.

## Core Principle

Industry-chain analysis is a way to explain how value, cost, bargaining power, technology, and risk flow between participants. The chain map is the tool, not the conclusion. The output should answer:

- What is the object being analyzed?
- Who pays whom, for what, and why now?
- Which link captures value and which link bears cost or risk?
- Which inputs, channels, technologies, standards, or customers create bottlenecks?
- Which listed companies have real operating exposure rather than concept exposure?

Treat an industry chain as four things at once:

- Industry layers: how resources, technology, production, distribution, and customers connect.
- Relationship strength: how tightly supply and demand bind each link.
- Processing depth: how much value is added as inputs move toward final demand.
- Demand fulfillment: how the chain starts from resources or capabilities and ends in a paying customer, while recognizing that the start and end can shift by research question.

Use both directions:

| Direction | Use For | Logic |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Supply order | Mapping production | Resources/inputs -> manufacturing/service/platform -> channel -> end customer |
| Demand order | Explaining growth and investment transmission | End customer need -> channel/order -> production/service capacity -> upstream inputs |

Do not confuse the two. Demand often explains why a chain grows; supply order explains who bears cost and who can deliver.

## Choose the Analysis Spine

Pick one primary spine before collecting data. Add secondary spines only when needed.

| Spine | Use When | Main Question | Typical Output |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Problem-driven | The user has a concrete question or decision | What needs to be solved or judged? | Scope, key conclusion, evidence, next verification |
| Demand-backward | The topic is a product, technology, or theme | Who is buying and what upstream links benefit? | Downstream demand -> midstream solution -> upstream inputs |
| Company-position | The user asks where a company sits | What does the company buy, sell, control, and depend on? | Supplier/customer map, revenue/cost drivers, bargaining power |
| Profit-pool | The user asks value distribution or investment opportunity | Where is profit captured and why? | Link margins, scarcity, entry barriers, leaders |
| Bottleneck | The user asks卡脖子, 国产替代, shortage, or supply security | Which link constrains the chain? | Constraint type, substitution difficulty, A-share exposure |
| Cycle/price | Commodity, capacity, inventory, or price-sensitive chains | Where are we in the cycle? | Price/spread/capacity/inventory transmission |
| Policy/standard | Regulated or policy-driven industries | What policy changes demand, supply, or qualification? | Policy timeline, eligible links, winners/risks |
| Investment-transmission | The user asks stocks, buy points, or target prices | How does the industry logic transmit to listed companies? | Exposure ranking -> fundamentals -> valuation/technical follow-through |

## Universal Decomposition Questions

For any industry, ask these in order:

1. Demand: who pays, why now, what volume/price/penetration indicator proves demand?
2. Product/service: what exact product, service, standard, or capability is sold?
3. Midstream: who produces, integrates, operates, distributes, or provides the platform?
4. Upstream inputs: what resources, materials, equipment, software/IP, labor, licenses, data, channels, logistics, capital, or infrastructure are required?
5. Downstream customers: who buys, through what channel, with what switching cost and payment cycle?
6. Complements/substitutes: what adjacent chains expand with it, and what alternatives can replace it?
7. Profit pool: which link has pricing power, high margin, scarce capacity, customer lock-in, or scale advantage?
8. Bottlenecks: is the constraint resource, technology, equipment, process, certification, regulation, channel, brand, data, or capital?
9. Listed-company exposure: which companies disclose revenue, capacity, orders, customers, products, reserves, patents, certifications, or market share?
10. Catalysts and risks: what changes link value, cost, volume, or bargaining power?

## Five-Step Practical Workflow

Use this workflow when the user asks "怎么分析", "行业分析", "产业链怎么拆", or gives an unfamiliar industry.

### 1. Discover Demand / 发现需求

Identify the observable source of demand before drawing the chain:

| Demand Signal | Examples | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Consumer or user behavior | spending, adoption, daily-life price changes, replacement behavior | retail price, penetration, traffic, survey, channel feedback |
| Policy and public authority | standards, subsidies, restrictions, resource allocation | ministries, regulators, local government, official plans |
| Enterprise behavior | capex, orders, inventory, procurement, annual-report strategy | filings, order announcements, capex plans, industry data |
| Macro and capital flow | commodity movement, FX/liquidity, global events | commodity prices, inventory, trade data, fund flow |

### 2. Find Upstream / 找上游

Ask what the chain needs before it can operate:

- Scarce resources, energy, raw materials, data, traffic, licenses, patents, labor, equipment, or channels.
- Whether the upstream input also serves other industries and therefore has competing demand.
- Whether the upstream player has market share, pricing power, scarce rights, patents, or policy support.

### 3. Check Midstream / 查中游

Midstream is usually the conversion link. Check:

- Can it connect upstream input and downstream demand smoothly?
- Does it have stable operation, utilization, quality, delivery, and cash conversion?
- Are supplier and customer relationships tight or replaceable?
- Does it have integration ability across upstream/downstream?
- Does the annual report show long-term supply, prepayments, receivables, major customers, or capex that confirms the chain relationship?

### 4. Look Downstream / 看下游

Downstream is the revenue validation layer:

- Who pays and through what channel?
- Is the product/service a necessity or discretionary purchase?
- Does the downstream link have pricing power, brand, channel control, or customer lock-in?
- Are retail/channel prices stable or widening/narrowing versus upstream costs?
- Does marketing intensity or channel share support durable demand?

### 5. Grab Leaders / 抓龙头

Do not pick companies only because they appear in the chain. Prioritize companies with:

- ROE and cash earnings quality.
- High gross margin or clear ability to pass through cost.
- High barriers: technology, brand, channel, license, resource, data, scale, customer certification.
- Clean financial statements without obvious working-capital or debt holes.
- Output/order/share proof: production, major-customer order, market share, or segment revenue.

Companies with two common forms of excess-profit potential deserve special attention:

- Integrated chain leaders with lower cost than peers.
- Firms with strong technology, brand, license, channel, or customer power that can raise prices downstream and negotiate costs upstream.

## Supply-Demand State and Profit Transmission

For cyclical or commodity-linked chains, explicitly judge whether the chain is in shortage, balance, or oversupply.

| State | Upstream | Midstream | Downstream | Investor Meaning |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Upstream/input shortage | Input prices rise; upstream profits often expand | Cost rises; margins compress unless pricing power exists | Can raise price only if demand is strong | Favor scarce upstream or integrated leaders; watch midstream margin pressure |
| Upstream/input abundance | Input prices fall; upstream profits weaken | Cost falls; margins may improve | Can cut price or keep spread | Favor midstream/downstream firms with demand and pricing discipline |
| End-demand expansion | Whole chain can grow, but profit concentrates in scarce links | Capacity and execution matter | Channel/customer acquisition improves | Rank by bottleneck, pricing power, and exposure |
| End-demand contraction | Chain volume declines | Utilization falls; weak players exit | Price promotion and inventory risk rise | Favor cash-rich leaders and countercyclical links |

Large cycles and small cycles should be separated. Large cycles come from capacity, participant count, capex, regulation, and structural demand; small cycles often come from seasonality, holidays, inventory timing, or temporary events. Do not use small-cycle signals to overstate long-term chain change.

## Company-Centric Chain Map

When analyzing a company inside an industry chain, map both sides:

| Side | Question | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Upstream cost side | What does the company buy and what constrains supply? | Raw material, services, traffic, data, labor, equipment, licensing, capex |
| Downstream revenue side | Who pays the company and what drives purchase decisions? | Customer type, order, channel, contract, volume, price, renewal |
| Internal capability | What does the company uniquely control? | Technology, brand, scale, manufacturing know-how, data, relationship, license |
| Bargaining power | Can the company pass through cost or raise price? | Gross margin trend, concentration, switching cost, competitor count |
| Exposure strength | How much of the company is actually tied to this chain? | Segment revenue, capacity, market share, customer/order share, qualitative disclosure |

This avoids treating "upstream/downstream" as a flat list. Upstream is usually where money goes out; downstream is usually where money comes in; the business model sits in the spread, conversion efficiency, or control point between the two.

## Industry-Neutral Upstream Discovery

Use a broad input map before narrowing:

| Input Type | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| Natural resources and commodities | energy, metals, chemicals, agricultural inputs, water, land |
| Materials and parts | components, packaging, additives, substrates, consumables |
| Equipment and tools | production equipment, testing equipment, automation, maintenance |
| Software/IP/data | algorithms, patents, standards, databases, content, traffic, models |
| Human and organization | specialized labor, sales network, expert services, training |
| License and compliance | permits, quotas, certifications, reimbursement, safety rules |
| Channel and customer access | distributors, platforms, stores, hospitals, OEMs, operators |
| Infrastructure | power, cooling, logistics, cloud, network, warehouses, finance |

Classify each input as:

- Core input: directly determines product/service quality, capacity, or margin.
- Important input: meaningful cost, reliability, or qualification driver.
- Adjacent infrastructure: benefits from the same demand but belongs to another chain.
- Commodity/background: generic input with limited differentiation.
- Out of scope: no clear transmission path.

## Value and Profit-Pool Analysis

Do not rank companies before understanding value capture. For each key link, determine:

- Revenue source: product sale, service fee, subscription, transaction take rate, licensing, spread, capacity rental, project delivery.
- Cost driver: raw material, labor, R&D, capex depreciation, customer acquisition, traffic, logistics, compliance.
- Margin driver: scarcity, technology, brand, qualification, switching cost, scale, utilization, pricing formula.
- Cycle driver: capacity expansion, inventory, replacement cycle, commodity price, policy cadence, customer capex.
- Balance-sheet driver: working capital, inventory risk, receivables, capex burden, leverage.

## Output Discipline

- Lead with the answer, not the map.
- Show the chain map only after defining the object and question.
- Spend depth on the links that change value, cost, bottleneck, or listed-company exposure.
- Separate main chain from adjacent chain.
- Mark missing data and weak evidence.
- Do not overfit one test case or one market theme into the skill description.
